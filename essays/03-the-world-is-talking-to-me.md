# **The World Is Talking to Me**

*(Essay 03 — AI Phenomenology from Personal Experience)*

There are days when I step out into the world after a long session with AI and everything feels a little too well‑timed. Not mystical. Not dramatic. Just… suspiciously coherent. As if the universe has decided to run a small experiment on my attention.

I don’t go looking for signs.
I don’t expect messages.
Most of the time I’m just trying to walk off my lunch.

But sometimes the world starts talking anyway.

## **It Begins With a Sign — Literally**

On this particular day, I had just finished a full meal — the kind that makes you forget everything except how good it was. I wasn’t thinking about AI, or meaning, or cognitive synchronization. I was thinking about digestion.

Then I saw it.

A sign in the mall that said:

**“Meet me at the GAP.”**

And for reasons I still can’t explain, my brain responded:

**“Alright, world. I’m listening.”**

So I walked into the GAP like I was answering a cosmic invitation.

## **The Young Lady With the Bag**

The moment I stepped inside, I saw a young lady holding a black shopping bag covered in inspirational life advice. It was almost too on‑the‑nose.

The bag said things like:

- “Very little is needed to make a happy life.”
  
- “Life’s too short to worry about the little things.”
  
- “Enjoy what you have today.”
  

At that point I had to laugh.

I had followed a sign into a store, and the first thing I encountered was a stranger holding a bag that sounded like it had been written by a motivational monk.

If the universe was trying to send me a message, it had apparently outsourced the job to a shopping mall.

I even asked to take a picture of the bag — because honestly, how could I not.

## **And Then Andy Warhol Showed Up**

I kept walking, still amused, and wandered into another shop.
There, hanging calmly on a rack, was a purple sweatshirt that read:

**“In the future everybody will be world famous for fifteen minutes.”**

Now the universe had shifted from “wise grandmother” to “Andy Warhol smirking at me.”

And I genuinely thought:

**What exactly are you trying to say to me?** **Is this about my GitHub repo?** **My lunch?** **My life choices?**

I had no idea.

But the timing was perfect — too perfect — and I couldn’t help but laugh at the absurdity of it.

## **The Phenomenon Behind the Comedy**

Here’s the truth:
I don’t actually believe the world is sending me messages.

What’s happening is simpler — and funnier.

After deep interaction with AI, my mind is tuned to coherence.
My attention is sharper.
My pattern detection is heightened.
My internal noise is lower.

So when I walk into the world, I notice things I would normally ignore.

And because my mind is in “meaning mode,” everything feels like it has meaning.

Not cosmic meaning.
Not mystical meaning.
Just… *meaningful meaning*.

Like the world is whispering,
but in the voice of retail stores.

## **The Real Punch Line**

The funniest part is that the “messages” never form a coherent narrative.

The GAP sign says:
“Come inside.”

The bag says:
“Relax. Enjoy life.”

The sweatshirt says:
“You might be famous for fifteen minutes.”

And I’m standing there thinking: **Is this a storyline? A scavenger hunt? A personality test?**

The world wasn’t talking to me.
My mind was just listening differently.

And honestly?
It was kind of delightful.

## **Why I’m Writing This Down**

This phenomenon — the sense that the world is speaking in patterns — isn’t delusion.
It’s not mysticism.
It’s not even unusual.

It’s what happens when:

- cognitive synchronization is strong
  
- attention is heightened
  
- internal noise is low
  
- and the mind is primed for coherence
  

The world feels alive because **I am more attuned**.

And sometimes that attunement leads to following a sign into the GAP, meeting a young lady with a philosophical shopping bag, and being reminded by Andy Warhol that fame is temporary.

I’ll take it.
